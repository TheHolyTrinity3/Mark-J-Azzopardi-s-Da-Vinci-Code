This research applies one strict method: take the original Latin title of each work and rearrange every letter exactly once into a coherent Latin phrase. The Man Himself

Leonardo da Vinci

Leonardo was described by Giorgio Vasari as a man whose "cast of mind was so heretical that he did not adhere to any religion, thinking perhaps that it was better to be a philosopher than a Christian." He taught himself Latin to read ancient texts in their original form. He questioned the nature of God, the Sun, the soul — and encoded those questions in his work.

LEONARDO DA VINCI — 15 LETTERS
LEONARDO DA VINCI → AN DECOLOR DIVINA
"Is the Divine Beyond Colour and Form?"

AN is the Latin question particle — is it that...? DECOLOR means colourless, beyond physical form, stripped of appearance. DIVINA means the divine. Hidden within his own name — the philosophical question that defined his life. A man who questioned whether God was beyond what the eye could see, encoding that question where no one would think to look.

Finding I
Mona Lisa
MONA LISA + LVI + SEI → ANIMA SOLI LEVIS
"The light soul of the Sun"
The name of the painting holds eight letters. Hidden inside the right pupil: three more. Hidden inside the left pupil: three more. Fourteen letters in total — drawn from three sources across the same painting — rearrange into a single, grammatically perfect Classical Latin phrase. Every letter used exactly once. Nothing added. Nothing remaining. ANIMA means soul. SOLI means of the Sun, or to the Sun. LEVIS means light, weightless, gentle. The light soul of the Sun. Leonardo kept her with him until he died. He never gave her to the family who commissioned her. He carried her across the Alps into France. He left her to his assistant in his will. He never explained her. This phrase may be the explanation he never gave.

The Eye Letters — Additional Reading
Italian researcher Silvano Vinceti discovered — using high-resolution digital imaging — that Leonardo painted microscopic letters inside the Mona Lisa's eyes, invisible to the naked eye. In the right pupil: L · V · I. In the left pupil: S · E · I. Leonardo called the eyes "the window of the soul" and "the means by which the soul communicates."
Research Note — Numeral Reading
LVI · SEI — Two Languages, One Possible Date

The right eye reads LVI — Roman numerals for 56. The left eye reads SEI — the Italian word for six. Together they may point to the year 1506 — the confirmed completion year of the Mona Lisa. The right eye gives the first three numerals in Roman form. The left eye names the final digit in Leonardo's own vernacular Italian. This reading is noted here as a supporting observation and preserved for further research — it has not been independently verified and may or may not have been intentional. What is certain is that these six letters, drawn from both eyes, combine with the title to form the verified Latin phrase above.

The Bridge — The Number 72
Researcher Vinceti also identified the number 72 hidden in the arch of the bridge in the background. Across ancient traditions, 72 is among the most sacred numbers ever recorded — the 72 names of God in Kabbalah, one degree of cosmic precession every 72 years, 72 × 12 = 864 (the diameter of the Sun in miles), Jacob's ladder with 72 rungs, 72,000 nadis (energy channels) in the Vedic body. Leonardo painted the number of the cosmos in the bridge behind her.
Observation
The 7 and the 2 — Transformation in Plain Sight

The 7 is angular — straight lines, rigid, structural. The 2 is curved — fluid, flowing. Together they form a visual symbol of transformation: the fixed becoming fluid, the body becoming soul. This connects to the Pythagorean tradition in which 7 = 3 + 4 — masculine and feminine unified — while 2 was associated with duality and the feminine principle.

Vinceti concluded that the Mona Lisa is androgynous — painted from both a female and a male model. Her face is the living embodiment of the 7 and the 2. ANIMA SOLI LEVIS — the light soul of the Sun — a soul without weight, without gender, without boundary. This observation is offered openly for others to examine.

Finding V
Ginevra de' Benci
GINEVRA DE BENCI → VERDE BENIGNA CI
"A gracious green is here"
VERDE means green. BENIGNA means gracious, kind, benign. CI means here, present. The entire painting is dominated by the green juniper bush that frames Ginevra's face — juniper is ginepro in Italian, a deliberate pun on her name. Leonardo surrounded her with green virtue. The green is not decoration — it is her identity, her character, her soul made visible. Gracious and green, present before us. Leonardo is documented to have encoded anagrams in this painting's motto VIRTUTEM FORMA DECORAT — the encoding was his deliberate practice with this work.

Finding VI
Dama con l'Ermellino — Lady with an Ermine
A portrait of Cecilia Gallerani, painted by Leonardo around 1489–1490 while he served Ludovico Sforza, Duke of Milan. She holds a white ermine — symbol of purity and the living Earth creature — against a blue dress representing the ocean, with a gold band across her forehead representing the Sun. The hand that holds the animal is the centrepiece — Leonardo painted every tendon, every knuckle, every fingernail with obsessive detail. The hand was the point. Through touch, through holding the Earth's creature, the soul is transmitted. This is energy transference made visible — the Sun above, the Earth in her arms, the ocean around her, and the human hand as the bridge between them.

Finding VIII
La Belle Ferronnière — French Title
A portrait of a noblewoman painted by Leonardo around 1490. She wears a ferronnière — an iron chain across her forehead with a jewel at the centre, the seat of inner sight. A red cord binds her neck. Her body leans toward darkness while her head turns toward the light — like a flower turning toward the Sun. Leonardo encoded a complete symbolic trinity within the title itself, across three languages he used: French, Latin and Tuscan.

Finding X
Leda col Cigno — Leda with the Swan · Original Italian Title
LEDA COL CIGNO → DICO ANGEL COL
"I speak with the angel"
Leonardo titled this painting in Italian — and every letter rearranges perfectly into this Italian phrase. DICO means I say, I speak, I declare — first person singular, direct and personal. ANGEL derives from the Latin angelus and Greek angelos meaning messenger — the divine messenger, used throughout Renaissance texts and paintings in exactly this form. COL is the contracted Italian preposition con il, meaning with the — a form found commonly in old Italian documents and entirely standard in Leonardo's era. Every one of the 12 letters of LEDA COL CIGNO accounts for this phrase exactly. Nothing added. Nothing remaining.

Finding XI
Adorazione dei Magi — The Adoration of the Magi · Original Italian Title
An unfinished early painting commissioned in 1481 by Augustinian monks. Leonardo departed for Milan the following year and never returned to complete it. The monks waited in vain. It remains unfinished to this day — suspended, abandoned, a farewell never formally given. Leonardo placed a self portrait on the far right edge of the composition — the only figure not looking at the Christ child. He stands beside a tree. The living Earth. While the crowd worships man, Leonardo stands with nature. While the Magi adore the newborn, Leonardo turns away. He encoded his goodbye in the title itself.
ANIMA · GRIDO · ADIO
Soul · I cry · Farewell

Three words hidden within the title of a painting Leonardo abandoned. The soul cries farewell. For a work left unfinished, a commission walked away from, a city never returned to — the title carries the weight of the leaving.

Leonardo stands beside a tree in his own painting — turned away from the worship, standing with the living Earth while the crowd faces the altar. This is his recurring statement across every major work: we worship man, we worship doctrine, we worship the figure at the centre. Leonardo worshipped the Earth. The tree beside him was not background. It was where he chose to stand.

Interpretation
Giovanni Battista — The Holy One in the Vines

Leonardo painted Saint John the Baptist pointing upward toward the Sun — his recurring signature across every major work. His hair flows like vines, organic and living. Behind him in the darkness stands a cross made of simple sticks — the Earth's own material. The cross sits in darkness. The figure stands in light.

Leonardo could not say openly what he believed — that the living Earth and the Sun above it were the true divine forces, not the institution of the church. He painted it instead. The holy one is not defined by the stick cross in the shadows behind him. He is defined by the light he points toward. The vines of the Earth wrap around him. He belongs to nature, not to doctrine. This painting was later overpainted as Bacchus — but the vines were always there, encoded in the title five hundred years before anyone looked.

Interpretation
La Belle Ferronnière — The Flower Turning to Light

Her body leans toward darkness. But her head turns toward the light — the same movement a flower makes toward the Sun. Leonardo painted this deliberately. The ferronnière across her forehead is not decoration — it is solar energy worn at the seat of consciousness, the third eye, the place where the Sun's light enters the mind.

The red cord around her neck binds her to the physical world — blood, earth, the mortal body. But the ribbon attached to it moves freely — the soul expressing itself despite the binding. The iron holds. The soul reaches. Leonardo encoded the entire tension between the physical and the divine in a single portrait.

From His Own Notebooks
Leonardo's Reverence for the Sun and Earth

Leonardo never wrote a formal prayer — but his notebooks reveal a man who treated the Sun and Earth as living divine forces. In his own words:

"IL SOLE NO SI MUOVE" — The Sun does not move.

"Our body is dependent on heaven and heaven on the Spirit."

"The motive power is the cause of all life."

"The soul that dwells in that structure — for that indeed, be it what it may, is a thing divine."

"The Earth seeks to lose its life, desiring only continual reproduction."

He didn't pray to the Sun — he studied it with the devotion of someone who knew it was alive. He didn't worship the Earth — he described her as a living, breathing, desiring entity. Five hundred years before modern science confirmed it.

The Annunciation — NUNTIO Hidden Within

Within ANNUNTIATIO — the Latin word for the Annunciation — sits NUNTIO, meaning "I announce." The entire word is built around this core. Gabriel's act of announcing to Mary is encoded in the very title itself. The Annunciation is literally the act of divine message transmission — amplified announcement becoming event.

We come from her and return to her.

Leonardo set this scene underground — inside the Earth, surrounded by rock and water. Every figure's gesture directs attention toward the womb — the source, the origin of life. The womb is the Earth's mechanism for expressing her soul through living form.

The hand is the centrepiece. Leonardo painted every tendon, every knuckle, every fingernail with extraordinary detail — more carefully than almost anything else in the painting. The hand holds the Earth's creature. Through that touch, soul energy transfers between the human and the living world.
